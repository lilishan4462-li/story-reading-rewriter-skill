# Rewrite Rules Reference

## Current Version

Current canonical version: M15.

M15 adds hard-boundary repair rules discovered during full-book testing:

- whole-book continuity before chapter-by-chapter generation;
- targeted repair instead of world-line regeneration;
- naming consistency;
- timeline layering;
- antagonist-network clarity;
- real-world survival pressure throughout;
- realistic threats instead of cartoon villain threats.

## Universal Rewrite Method

### 1. Separate book structure from rewrite material

Do not automatically rewrite everything in the source file.

- Front matter, title page, copyright page, catalog, preface, recommendations, translator notes, and author self-introductions usually inform the rewrite but do not become story chapters.
- The actual body chapters become knowledge gates.
- If the source book has parts or sections, use them to shape acts.

### 2. Build a content outline first

Before producing many chapters, define:

- protagonist: name, age, job, weakness, desire, fear, real-life pressure;
- central mystery or pursuit;
- antagonist force: one visible face plus a clear network;
- supporting cast: each person must serve plot, cost, or knowledge;
- four-act structure;
- clue chain and recurring objects;
- character arc;
- knowledge arc;
- forbidden names, forbidden organizations, and existing established facts.

Do not generate later chapters without checking the content outline and prior published chapters.

### 3. Convert concepts into conflicts

For each source chapter:

1. Extract the core concept.
2. Identify the false interpretation.
3. Create a scene where the protagonist is tempted by the false interpretation.
4. Force a choice with cost.
5. Let the correct concept appear through consequence.
6. Reveal the concept after the story, not before.

### 4. Write for continuation

Each chapter should contain:

- a grounded opening scene;
- a concrete objective;
- a pressure source;
- at least one conflict;
- at least one cost-bearing choice;
- a clue or reveal;
- a final-scene hook embedded in action.

Avoid standalone moral fables. The chapter must move the whole-book plot.

## M15 Hard-Boundary Rules

### Naming consistency

- Direct relatives should not share characters unless there is a deliberate cultural reason.
- A junior name must not look like a simplified elder name.
- If a character has a legal name, nickname, alias, or code name, explain it in-story.
- Do not change a character's name between chapters without explanation.

### Timeline clarity

- Identify early old cases versus recent events.
- Repeat the distinction where readers may confuse them.
- Avoid relative-only phrasing when exact dates matter.
- Fix impossible durations.

### Antagonist network clarity

- Give the antagonist one visible face.
- Clarify which characters are superiors, subordinates, intermediaries, protectors, or executors.
- Do not let the same label drift between "one person" and "an organization" unless explicitly defined.
- Reuse pressure from the visible antagonist even when subordinates act.

### Real survival pressure

Keep returning to ordinary costs:

- salary;
- rent;
- family safety;
- business survival;
- public reputation;
- legal or procedural pressure;
- archive access;
- time deadlines;
- medical or care burdens.

### Realistic threats

Prefer:

- reports and inspections;
- account freezing;
- eviction pressure;
- workplace complaints;
- public-smear campaigns;
- archive tampering;
- procedural delays;
- engineered accidents with plausible deniability.

Avoid cartoon villain lines such as direct explosions, kidnapping speeches, or "I will destroy you" unless the genre and established antagonist justify them.

### Targeted repair

When a chapter has problems:

- fix the smallest necessary passage;
- preserve the protagonist, world, plot, and established facts;
- do not re-roll the chapter and risk introducing a new world line;
- compare before and after for continuity.

## Knowledge Reveal Standard

Every chapter keeps a knowledge reveal after the story.

Required elements:

1. Concept name and core meaning.
2. Story mapping.
3. Real-life application.
4. Easy mistake.
5. One memorable sentence.
6. One reality-check question.

Keep it short. If the reveal feels like a slide deck, reduce explanation and keep only the concept's usable core.
